Ground Rules

Be open-minded. We may introduce ideas in the training that you are unsure about or have questions about. We ask that you stay open-minded to us and to your fellow mentors.

Be willing to be uncomfortable. We like to think of mentoring as something that is positive, and it is. Yet being an effective mentor means we may have to be uncomfortable with what we don’t know. It can also mean trying new things, or working with other mentors and with student mentees in ways that push us out of our comfort zone. This is when we learn. It doesn’t mean it is easy.

Speak from your own perspective. We want you to use “I” statements, which means you speak for yourself and from your own vantage point. It’s easy to say “we at Microsoft” or “we from this university” think or do things in a certain way. But, it helps if you only speak for yourself rather than for other people.

Be patient and allow space for all to learn. We recognize everyone is at different places in their learning and have had different experiences. For some of you, this may be a review, and you could facilitate training for others. We ask you to be patient and help others move forward. For this pilot to be successful, we will need everyone to be on the same page and at a certain level of comfort. Toward that end, please think about whether you should step up or step back. If you are someone who needs to push yourself and learn more, we encourage you to step up to the challenge. If someone in your group needs you for support, we ask you to step up to this challenge. If you are someone who knows the answer or knows what to do, please step back from time to time so others get a chance to participate.

Facilitation means interruption. We only have a limited amount of time with you during each structured session. As facilitators, we may need to interrupt your thought process and conversation. Please know we value your contributions, and we do not interrupt for a lack of care for what you have to offer. To respect your time, we will push forward, which can feel challenging. We name this now to flag it going in. Please know you will have opportunities to ask questions or share ideas, whether in the chat, through end-of-session exit cards, after the session and in other spaces.

What’s said here stays here; what’s learned can be shared. When people are learning, they may make missteps. We all say things that we wish we could revise. We ask that you do not leave the training space and refer back to what specific people said when they were practicing their mentoring. This way we can all relax a bit into learning. We do, however, encourage you to share anything you learn.

Active listening: speak and listen

Duration: 5 minutes

  1. Introduce yourself: your name and pronouns, if you want to be referred to using pronouns.
  2. Decide who will go first.
  3. Choose one mentoring story to share. Talk about someone who influenced you early on in your own CS or tech journey. This can be someone who had a positive or negative influence, but ideally someone you saw as a potential mentor. What did they do or say that stuck with you?
  4. Speaker: Take 90 seconds to share your mentoring story.
  5. Listener: Please do not interrupt when someone is sharing their story. You are practicing active listening. Take 30 seconds to say what you heard and what you took from the story. You can mirror with phrases like: “I heard you say…” or “It sounded like…”
  6. [If you are in a trio] Observer: Listen and keep time.
  7. Switch roles.

Clarifying your mentor role(s)

Boundaries and Professionalism

In this mentoring program,
  • Mentors are facilitators.
    You will share your stories. You will lead students through activities and offer insights to them. You will encourage students to share their experiences, and actively listen as they share. You will be responsible for moving the group through the mentoring program so you should show up regularly and on time for the program.
  • Mentors are coaches.
    You will coach students by providing advice and serving as a sounding board. You will help them to leverage the resources to which they have access. You are not their teachers, tutors, or career development specialists. However, students may ask you to help fill in the gaps of what they are not getting from these individuals or to unpack what they are learning or experiencing. This may require some negotiation on the part of the mentor as you clarify your boundaries with the participating students.
  • Mentors are responsible role models.
    You are responsible for setting the tone for an inclusive mentoring space. Your words and behaviors matter. Aspire mentors are expected to uphold the same professional behaviors expected when you are at work at Microsoft. For student Learn Ambassadors, you are expected to uphold the same professional expectations of any peer leader on campus. You and your co-mentor will work together to create a space where everyone feels respected and valued. You will enlist the mentoring cohort of students to join you.
  • Mentors are committed to learning.
    We will share more resources and information about this as we get closer to the program launch. For example, Aspire mentors will find out which campus they have been assigned to and more about their student cohort. You will find out more about what mentors can keep confidential and what they must report to the program leaders. We will address more behavioral expectations that pertain to this program.

Video share: Can I do this? (self-efficacy)

Duration: 8 minutes

What resonated?
  • You can reflect on an element in the video that spoke to you or made you think of something that happened in your own life.
  • Take a minute to write this down for yourself. Then you'll be able to focus on what the other person is saying (rather than thinking about what you want to say).
  • Practice similar active listening strategies as you did in the opening exercise. Please share the air time. Try to avoid talking for more than 1 minute at a time so the other person can respond or share their experience.
  • Constructive ways to respond: “Thanks for sharing that with me.” “I enjoyed talking to you.” “You gave me something to think about.”

Lesson: crafting a story

Tips for a productive conversation

  • Do clarify (mirror) what you hear: “I’m hearing you say that… Did I get that right?”
  • Do appreciate others for sharing: “Thanks so much for sharing that with me.”
  • Do feel that you can change your mind: “I originally thought ____ but now I think differently.”
  • Don’t ask someone to represent an identity group. For example, do not ask: “As the only ___ in our group, could you suggest how to fix this?”
    • By asking someone to represent their group, you are unfairly asking someone to serve in that role to suit your needs. You need to let people choose which identities they bring forward for themselves.
    • Instead, you can say: “We need to understand a range of perspectives on this. Personally I know that I need to do some work to learn more about race.”
    • Speak from your own place of concern: “I feel concerned about making a misstep in this area.”
  • Don’t put down another person’s ideas even if you disagree. For example, do not say: “That’s a terrible idea, that wouldn’t work.” or “You really don’t know what you are talking about.”
    • People may have had different experiences from you. It’s ok for you to disagree, and you can find a way to bring your perspective forward. At the same time, this training is a time to be open and hear from others; maybe there is a way of thinking you haven’t considered. If you shut down other people, you can’t expect them to listen to you when you weigh in.
    • Instead, you can say: “It sounds like your experience is quite different than mine -- would you be willing to share more?” or “Hm, I am not sure I see that perspective. Can you say more about how that would work?”

Scenario Activity

Duration: 15 minutes

The following scenarios are intended to spark a conversation about crafting a story to share with their cohort. Read each scenario and use the following prompts to guide your discussion:
  • What might you choose to keep or change if this was your story to tell? (You may want to consider intent vs. impact.)
  • How might you share a story that complements this mentor’s story if you were the co-mentor?

Scenario A
In preparing to lead into the video “Can I do this?” on self-efficacy (perception of ability), a mentor is struggling to craft their story. Their starting point is below, but they’re unsure of how to make their story relevant, as they’ve never struggled in tech.
I know looking back the reality is much of the tech learning came easily to me. I did not have to work hard like my peers. Growing up, I did have all of the latest gadgets. I was surrounded by tech. The real struggle for me came from being impatient with my classmates. I got frustrated easily and had to realize not everyone knew everything quickly like me.
Scenario B
In preparing to lead into the video “Is this for me?” on belongingness (sense of feeling valued and respected), a mentor is not sure how to share their story. The reality is that experience is still very raw, and every time they think about it, they get very emotional and upset. They’re worried they may scare away the mentees and/or break down when telling it.
During my first internship, my manager was mean and consistently shut me down. One time I had my code raked over the coals in front of the whole team. When I started to cry, my manager laughed and told me to toughen up. I’ll never forget that moment and how badly I wanted to leave. The tech industry will eat you alive if you let it. To be honest, I still struggle every day with whether I chose the right field.
