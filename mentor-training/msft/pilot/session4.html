Ground Rules

Be open-minded. We may introduce ideas in the training that you are unsure about or have questions about. We ask that you stay open-minded to us and to your fellow mentors.

Be willing to be uncomfortable. We like to think of mentoring as something that is positive, and it is. Yet being an effective mentor means we may have to be uncomfortable with what we don’t know. It can also mean trying new things, or working with other mentors and with student mentees in ways that push us out of our comfort zone. This is when we learn. It doesn’t mean it is easy.

Speak from your own perspective. We want you to use “I” statements, which means you speak for yourself and from your own vantage point. It’s easy to say “we at Microsoft” or “we from this university” think or do things in a certain way. But, it helps if you only speak for yourself rather than for other people.

Be patient and allow space for all to learn. We recognize everyone is at different places in their learning and have had different experiences. For some of you, this may be a review, and you could facilitate training for others. We ask you to be patient and help others move forward. For this pilot to be successful, we will need everyone to be on the same page and at a certain level of comfort. Toward that end, please think about whether you should step up or step back. If you are someone who needs to push yourself and learn more, we encourage you to step up to the challenge. If someone in your group needs you for support, we ask you to step up to this challenge. If you are someone who knows the answer or knows what to do, please step back from time to time so others get a chance to participate.

Facilitation means interruption. We only have a limited amount of time with you during each structured session. As facilitators, we may need to interrupt your thought process and conversation. Please know we value your contributions, and we do not interrupt for a lack of care for what you have to offer. To respect your time, we will push forward, which can feel challenging. We name this now to flag it going in. Please know you will have opportunities to ask questions or share ideas, whether in the chat, through end-of-session exit cards, after the session and in other spaces.

What’s said here stays here; what’s learned can be shared. When people are learning, they may make missteps. We all say things that we wish we could revise. We ask that you do not leave the training space and refer back to what specific people said when they were practicing their mentoring. This way we can all relax a bit into learning. We do, however, encourage you to share anything you learn.

Group Mentoring Sessions: Participation Strategies

  • Group Polls: Each participant gives an anonymous opinion by clicking on questions on the screen. This allows the group to engage without hand-raising or speaking.
  • Pair-Share: Participants break into pairs where each person shares with a partner about their experience and actively listens to their partner's experience. It is typical for the pairs to come back to the large group and a member of the pair shares out to the larger group. Many who find large groups difficult will participate in a pair-share activity. A pair-share can be done with trios.
  • Round Robin: This method of participation is typically saved for the end of a structured group session, before moving into open discussion. Each person is called upon to share, and can pass their turn. Some may choose to defer (come back to them), or skip their turn (do not participate). This method helps all voices to be heard.
    Because we’re in a remote setting, I’m going to just call on you, but you can pass.
    X, we’d love to hear from you. Unmute yourself. Can you take a minute or so to tell us what resonated?
  • Active listening: listen to understand
  • Reflect back and synthesize: mirror what you heard and highlight common themes or salient differences
  • Step up, step back: be aware of when to take up space, and when to make room for more voices

Can I open up the space?

Tips for facilitation

  • Remember to practice active listening.
  • As a facilitator, practice round robin (or “selected round robin” on a subset of the group), reflecting back and synthesizing (as in Self-Guided Activity #4)
    • Reflect back: When your cohort shares their experiences, you mirror or restate in your own words what you heard.
    • Rather than comment after each person shares (aside from thanking them or clarifying what they said), hear back from each member first.
    • Synthesize across the group: Highlight themes/commonalities (“it is interesting that all of you found this component of the lesson to be so valuable.”) or contrasts/differences (“I noticed that this really resonated with some of you, while others of you felt less connected to it.”)

Tips for a productive conversation

  • Do clarify (mirror) what you hear: “I’m hearing you say that… Did I get that right?”
  • Do appreciate others for sharing: “Thanks so much for sharing that with me.”
  • Do feel that you can change your mind: “I originally thought ____ but now I think differently.”
  • Don’t ask someone to represent an identity group. For example, do not ask: “As the only ___ in our group, could you suggest how to fix this?”
    • By asking someone to represent their group, you are unfairly asking someone to serve in that role to suit your needs. You need to let people choose which identities they bring forward for themselves.
    • Instead, you can say: “We need to understand a range of perspectives on this. Personally I know that I need to do some work to learn more about race.”
    • Speak from your own place of concern: “I feel concerned about making a misstep in this area.”
  • Don’t put down another person’s ideas even if you disagree. For example, do not say: “That’s a terrible idea, that wouldn’t work.” or “You really don’t know what you are talking about.”
    • People may have had different experiences from you. It’s ok for you to disagree, and you can find a way to bring your perspective forward. At the same time, this training is a time to be open and hear from others; maybe there is a way of thinking you haven’t considered. If you shut down other people, you can’t expect them to listen to you when you weigh in.
    • Instead, you can say: “It sounds like your experience is quite different than mine -- would you be willing to share more?” or “Hm, I am not sure I see that perspective. Can you say more about how that would work?”

Practice #1: Story activity

Duration: 15 minutes

This activity has 2 components: simulation and reflection. The lead facilitator for your group has an * in front of their name and will lead the group through the activity.

  1. Introduce yourselves with names and pronouns, if you want to be referred to using pronouns.
  2. Prepare for the simulation
    1. The lead facilitator (*) will play the role of Mentor A facilitating a video module. If you prepared for both the feedback and mindset videos, simulate feedback.
    2. Select a co-mentor B -- Mentor B will keep track of time (and lead the reflection round).
    3. Select two mentees.
    4. Everyone else is an observer.
  3. [8 minutes] Run the simulation, facilitated by Mentor A, with Mentor B chiming in to keep to time.
    1. [2 minutes] Mentor A: Lead-in to the video with your prepared story.
    2. Note that typically the video would be shown in the group mentoring session, which is skipped for this simulation.
    3. [4 minutes] Mentor A: Round robin with the mentees.
      You’ll each have about a minute to share what resonated with you. Since we're in a remote session, I'll call on you by name. Remember, you can pass.
    4. [2 minutes] Mentor A: Reflect back and synthesize.
  4. Reflect on the simulation with a discussion facilitated by Mentor B. We recommended starting with a round robin with the observers.
    Now we’ll debrief the activity. Take a moment to reflect -- how was that for you? What might you have done differently if you were the mentor? Did anyone get shortchanged?

Practice #2: Pivot activity

Duration: 25 minutes

This activity has 2 components: pivot prompts and discussion. Remember, the lead facilitator for your group has an * in front of their name and will lead the group through the activity.

  1. Prepare for the pivot prompts.
    The lead facilitator acts as narrator. There will be 3 pivot prompts, each to be discussed for 3 minutes by a single pair while everyone else observes. Start by assigning the 3 pairs; observers from the last round should be assigned first. Depending on your group size, you can assign yourself to one pair and/or may need someone to participate twice.
  2. Rotate through the pivot prompts.
    1. [3 minutes] Prompt Pair 1 to discuss Pivot Prompt 1 by reading it out loud to them, while others listen in. Ask an observer to keep time.

      Pivot Prompt 1

      You are having a really rotten day. You think about canceling on the mentoring group, but decide to show up. When you call on people to contribute, you notice one student is looking at their phone. They don’t even hear you call their name. You really want to give them a piece of your mind, given all that you are giving up to serve as a mentor in this program.
    2. [3 minutes] Prompt Pair 2 to discuss Pivot Prompt 2 by reading it out loud to them, while others listen in. Ask an observer to keep time.

      Pivot Prompt 2

      It is now the second session. You notice that two of the students in the group, even though they are technically “your” mentees, only seem to address your co-mentor. It’s as if you are not even in the session.
    3. [3 minutes] Prompt Pair 3 to discuss Pivot Prompt 3 by reading it out loud to them, while others listen in. Ask an observer to keep time.

      Pivot Prompt 3

      Your co-mentor cuts you off each time you start to answer a question. The students seem to notice, but your co-mentor is oblivious. At the end of the session, the two of you stay on to debrief. Your co-mentor says, “That was such a great session!”
  3. Discuss. Have an open discussion, returning to what you heard from each pair, and wondered about as they talked. You do not need to go in any particular Pivot Prompt order. You can also reflect on: What did you gain from hearing different perspectives?
